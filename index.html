Product Knowledge
📋
Term Life Insurance
Core Product
▾

What it is

Term life provides a death benefit for a set period — typically 10, 20, or 30 years. It is the most affordable and straightforward life insurance product. If the insured passes during the term, the beneficiary receives the face amount tax-free.

Who it's for

  • Young families needing income replacement
  • Homeowners with a mortgage
  • Business owners with key-person exposure
  • Anyone who needs maximum coverage at the lowest cost

Key selling points

  • Lowest premium for the highest death benefit
  • Simple to understand — easy to explain and close
  • Many carriers offer living benefits (critical illness, chronic illness riders) at no extra cost
  • Convertible term can be upgraded to permanent later

Sales Psychology

Lead with protection. Ask: "If something happened to you tomorrow, would your family be okay financially?" Most people say no. Term gives them peace of mind at a price that feels manageable. Anchor against their monthly expenses — $250,000 in coverage for less than a Netflix subscription per day.

Objection Handling

  • "I'll think about it" — "I understand. Let me ask — what specifically would you want to think about?" Then address that one concern directly.
  • "It's too expensive" — Reframe to daily cost. A $500K policy might be $28/month — less than a dinner out.
  • "I have coverage through work" — "Group coverage typically ends when you leave. Is that a risk you're comfortable with?"
📋
Whole Life Insurance
Permanent Protection
▾

What it is

Whole life is permanent coverage that lasts a lifetime. It builds guaranteed cash value over time that the policyholder can borrow against. Premiums are fixed and never increase. The death benefit is guaranteed as long as premiums are paid.

Who it's for

  • Final expense and burial planning (smaller policies $5K–$25K)
  • Parents buying for children to lock in low rates early
  • High-income earners using it as a tax-advantaged savings vehicle
  • Business succession planning

Key selling points

  • Coverage never expires — guaranteed for life
  • Cash value grows tax-deferred
  • Fixed premiums — no surprises
  • Dividends (participating policies) can increase cash value or reduce premiums

Sales Psychology

Whole life sells on permanence and certainty. Use the phrase: "Term is renting, whole life is owning." Clients who have had term expire appreciate this. For final expense, make it emotional: "This covers your funeral so your family only has to grieve — not worry about money."

📋
Indexed Universal Life (IUL)
Premium Product · High Commission
▾

What it is

IUL is a flexible permanent life insurance product where the cash value growth is tied to a stock market index (like the S&P 500) with a floor (typically 0%) so you never lose money in a down market, and a cap or participation rate on the upside. It offers tax-free growth and tax-free distributions in retirement.

Who it's for

  • Business owners and high-income earners who have maxed their 401k/IRA
  • Clients who want market-like growth without market risk
  • People planning for tax-free retirement income
  • Parents funding a child's future tax-free

Key selling points

  • Zero floor — you never lose cash value when markets drop
  • Tax-free growth and distributions via policy loans
  • Flexible premiums and adjustable death benefit
  • Living benefits built in on most carriers
  • Not subject to 401k contribution limits

Sales Psychology

IUL sells best as a retirement and wealth tool first, life insurance second. Ask: "Would you like to have a bucket of money in retirement that you can pull from tax-free, that never loses value in a market crash, and also protects your family if something happens?" The answer is always yes. Then connect it to their specific retirement concern.

Common Objections

  • "It sounds complicated" — Simplify: "It's like a savings account that grows with the market but has a safety net. You participate in the upside and are protected on the downside."
  • "I already have a 401k" — "Great. This complements it by giving you tax diversification — your 401k is taxed on the way out, this is not."
📋
Annuities
Retirement Income
▾

What it is

An annuity is a contract with an insurance carrier where a client deposits a lump sum and the carrier guarantees either growth, income, or both in return. Fixed Indexed Annuities (FIAs) are the most commonly sold — they offer index-linked growth with a floor of zero, and optional income riders that guarantee lifetime income.

Types to know

  • Fixed Annuity — Guaranteed interest rate, no market risk. Simple, predictable.
  • Fixed Indexed Annuity (FIA) — Growth tied to index with 0% floor. Most popular for agents.
  • Variable Annuity — Requires securities license. Avoid unless licensed.

Who it's for

  • Pre-retirees (age 50–65) rolling over 401k, IRA, or pension
  • Anyone afraid of outliving their money
  • Clients who want guaranteed income they cannot outlive

Sales Psychology

Annuities solve the #1 retirement fear: "I'm going to run out of money." Position as a personal pension. Ask: "What if I could show you a way to guarantee that you receive a paycheck every single month for the rest of your life — no matter how long you live — and you never have to worry about market crashes wiping it out?"

The 5-Step Sales Process

Master this process and every appointment becomes consistent, professional, and closable. Click each step to expand.

1
Introduction — Build Rapport & Credibility
Match energy · Build trust
▾

People buy from people they trust. Sales is a transference of energy — your belief, your confidence, and your enthusiasm transfer directly to the client. Match their energy first, then lead them.

  • Greet warmly and use their first name naturally
  • Match the client's energy: Relaxed? Be relaxed. Direct? Be direct. People trust people who feel like them
  • Credibility building: Mention LLFG as an FMO with access to top carriers — Mutual of Omaha, Transamerica, Aetna. You represent multiple divisions so you find the best rate for their situation
  • Carrier partners: Name 2–3 recognizable carriers. Clients feel reassured by household names
  • Insurance division: Explain you work across life, health, and retirement — you can address multiple needs
  • Overpayments alert: "One of the first things I do is check whether you're overpaying for coverage you already have." Positions you as an advisor, not a salesperson
  • High-risk health class alerts: Let clients know you work with carriers who specialize in conditions like diabetes, heart issues, or tobacco use — many clients have been declined or overcharged elsewhere
  • Smile, be present, and listen more than you talk
2
Fact Finder — Discover Coverage Needs & Budget
Diagnose before you prescribe
▾

This is the most important step. You cannot prescribe before you diagnose. Ask questions, listen carefully, and take notes.

  • Coverage questions: Do they have current coverage? When does it expire? What does it cover?
  • Family/income questions: Who depends on you financially? What debts need to be paid?
  • Goal questions: Protecting family? Building wealth? Planning retirement?
  • Budget anchoring: "Most clients invest between $80–$200/month. Does that feel like a range that works?"
  • Use what they share to match the product — don't sell, match
3
Application — SSN, Medical Underwriting & Banking
Accuracy protects your commission
▾

Guide them through the application professionally. Keep the energy positive — this is not bureaucracy, this is protecting their family.

  • Personal info: Full legal name, DOB, address, phone, email
  • Social Security Number: Required by the carrier for identity verification and underwriting
  • Medical underwriting: Tobacco, height, weight, health conditions, medications. Frame it as the carrier wanting to find the best rate for their health class
  • Banking info: Routing and account number. "Most clients prefer the 1st or 15th — which works better?" ⚠ Social Security clients: They receive payment on the 3rd — always draft the 5th or 6th so funds have cleared
  • Beneficiary: Full name, relationship, DOB, percentage split if multiple
  • Read all information back before submitting — one wrong digit causes a chargeback
4
Close — Thank, Ask for Referrals & Set Policy Expectations
Solidify the relationship
▾

The application is in. Now solidify the relationship, plant seeds for referrals, and prepare the client for what comes next.

  • Thank them sincerely: "I genuinely appreciate you trusting me with something this important."
  • Ask for referrals: "Who else do you know — a family member, coworker, or close friend — who would benefit from a conversation like this?"
  • Policy cancellation window: Explain the free-look period (usually 30 days). If they need to cancel they can do so at no cost — knowing this actually makes them less likely to use it
  • Confirm first draft date and what will appear on their bank statement
  • Leave them feeling confident and cared for — not sold to
5
Follow-Up — Referrals, Retention & Next Check-In
Where most agents fail — be the one who doesn't
▾

The sale doesn't end at the close. Your follow-up determines whether you keep the client long-term and whether they refer others to you.

  • 48-hour call: Confirm policy was submitted, answer questions, check in on how they're feeling
  • Policy delivery call: Walk them through what they received — coverage amounts, beneficiary, draft date
  • Ask for referrals again: "Now that your policy is in place, have you thought of anyone who might benefit from getting covered too?"
  • Cancellation watch: Monitor for NSF or missed drafts — call the same day if a payment fails. Don't wait for the carrier to lapse the policy
  • Next check-in: "I'd like to check in in about 6 months to make sure your coverage still makes sense as life changes." Opens doors for additional products
  • Send a handwritten thank-you card or personal message within a week
Sales Psychology Fundamentals
🧠
The Psychology of Buying Decisions
Core Concept
▾

People buy emotionally, justify logically

The decision to buy insurance is never purely rational. It is driven by fear (what if something happens?), love (protecting the people I care about), and identity (I am someone who is responsible). Your job is to connect the product to those emotions first — then provide the logical justification they need to feel good about the decision.

The Pain-Pleasure Framework

Every purchase is either moving toward pleasure or away from pain. Insurance leans heavily toward pain avoidance. Surface their pain: financial stress on family, debt left behind, funeral costs, lost income. Then position the policy as the specific solution that removes that pain permanently.

Trust before persuasion

People do not buy from people they don't trust. Spend more time building genuine rapport in the first 5 minutes of every appointment than on any pitch. Ask about their family, listen carefully, mirror their language, and never rush. Once trust exists, the sale becomes a conversation instead of a presentation.

Urgency without pressure

Real urgency comes from truth: rates increase with age, health can change without warning, and no one is guaranteed tomorrow. You don't need to manufacture pressure — just be honest. "The rate you qualify for today is based on your health today. If that changes, so does the rate. The best time to lock this in is now."

The takeaway close

If a client is hesitant, instead of pushing harder, pull back slightly: "It sounds like this might not be the right time for you — I understand completely. I just want to make sure you're protected. What would need to be true for you to feel comfortable moving forward?" This removes pressure and invites them to tell you their real objection.

B.O.L.T. — Client Personality Framework

B.O.L.T. stands for Bulls, Owls, Lambs, and Tigers — a framework for identifying a client's personality and communication style so you can adapt your approach in real time. The most successful agents are chameleons: they read the room, mirror the client's style, and adjust their energy, pace, and language accordingly. One size never fits all in sales.

🐂
B — Bull
Direct · Dominant · Results-Driven
▾

Who they are

Bulls are decisive, confident, and results-oriented. They don't want to waste time and they don't want to be over-explained to. They make decisions quickly and respect people who are equally direct. They are often business owners, executives, or anyone accustomed to being in charge.

How to spot them

  • They speak fast and get to the point quickly
  • They may interrupt or finish your sentences
  • They ask direct questions: "What's this going to cost me?" or "How does this work — bottom line?"
  • They project authority and confidence in their posture and tone

How to sell to a Bull

  • Be direct and confident — do not over-explain or ramble
  • Lead with the bottom line: what it costs, what they get, and why it makes sense now
  • Skip the small talk unless they initiate it — respect their time
  • Present options but make a clear recommendation: "Here's what I recommend for your situation and why."
  • Use data and logic — ROI, cost-per-day, coverage to premium ratio
  • Never be soft or apologetic — match their confidence level

What to avoid

Do not repeat yourself, hedge, or be vague. Bulls lose patience with agents who seem unsure. If they push back, hold your ground respectfully — they actually respect people who don't cave immediately.

🦉
O — Owl
Analytical · Detail-Oriented · Methodical
▾

Who they are

Owls are thinkers. They want to understand everything before making a decision. They ask detailed questions, want documentation, and may take notes. They are often engineers, accountants, doctors, or anyone with an analytical mindset. They make excellent clients once sold — they rarely cancel because they've thoroughly researched and committed.

How to spot them

  • They ask very specific, detailed questions: carrier ratings, exclusions, policy language
  • They speak slowly and deliberately
  • They may say "I need to think about it" or "Can I see the policy details?"
  • They are not impulsive — they weigh everything carefully

How to sell to an Owl

  • Slow down and match their pace — do not rush
  • Give them data: AM Best ratings, carrier history, sample illustrations
  • Explain the "why" behind every recommendation
  • Offer to send documents, product sheets, or carrier information for review
  • Acknowledge their questions positively: "That's a great question — here's exactly how that works."
  • Build in a natural next step: "Let me put together the details and we can review them together."

What to avoid

Never pressure an Owl. Rushing them will cause them to shut down entirely. Be patient — their thoroughness means once they commit, they stay committed.

🐑
L — Lamb
Gentle · People-Pleasing · Relationship-First
▾

Who they are

Lambs are warm, friendly, and relationship-driven. They want to like you before they do business with you. They are empathetic, non-confrontational, and may agree with you even when they have reservations — which is why follow-through is essential. They are often caregivers, teachers, or people who make decisions based on how something feels emotionally.

How to spot them

  • Very friendly, warm, and easy to talk to from the start
  • They ask personal questions and want to know about you
  • They say yes easily — but may back out later if they feel unsure
  • They are motivated by protecting their family and being responsible

How to sell to a Lamb

  • Invest in genuine rapport — this is not optional with a Lamb
  • Lead with the emotional story: family protection, legacy, peace of mind
  • Use testimonials or personal examples: "I had a client in a similar situation — here's what they did."
  • Validate their feelings and concerns warmly throughout
  • Make it easy and comfortable: "I'll walk you through every step — you don't need to worry about anything."
  • Follow up promptly and personally — they value the relationship

What to avoid

Do not be high-pressure. Lambs will smile, say yes, and then call to cancel the next day if they felt pushed. The key is genuine care — they can sense inauthenticity immediately. Build the relationship first; the sale follows naturally.

🐯
T — Tiger
Expressive · Enthusiastic · Social
▾

Who they are

Tigers are energetic, expressive, and love people. They are talkers, storytellers, and often the life of the room. They make decisions based on excitement and connection rather than pure logic. They love new ideas and possibilities. They are often entrepreneurs, salespeople, entertainers, or anyone in a high-energy social role.

How to spot them

  • They dominate the conversation enthusiastically
  • They tell stories and go off topic — and they love when you engage with them
  • They are expressive with their hands, voice, and energy
  • They get excited easily but may not follow through without a structured close

How to sell to a Tiger

  • Match their energy — be enthusiastic, expressive, and engaging
  • Let them talk and genuinely listen — they love being heard
  • Sell the vision and the story, not just the features: "Imagine knowing your family is completely taken care of no matter what happens."
  • Make them feel special and understood
  • Use urgency tied to excitement: "The rate you qualify for right now is excellent — let's lock it in today."
  • Guide them firmly to the close — Tigers can get distracted by conversation

What to avoid

Don't be boring or overly technical. Tigers will tune out fast if the energy drops. Keep it dynamic, keep it personal, and make sure you direct the conversation back to the close — otherwise you'll have a great conversation and no sale.

🦎
Be the Chameleon
The Master Sales Principle
▾

What it means

A chameleon adapts its environment to survive and thrive. The best agents in this industry do exactly the same thing. You are not one personality — you are a professional who can read any room and become what that client needs you to be in order to serve them best.

How to develop it

  • Listen in the first 60 seconds: Before you say almost anything, listen to how they speak. Fast or slow? Warm or direct? Detailed or broad? That tells you almost everything about how to proceed
  • Mirror their pace: If they speak slowly, slow down. If they're quick, pick up your pace. Pace mismatch is one of the most common reasons clients disengage without knowing why
  • Mirror their language: Use the words they use. If they say "protect my family," use "protect your family" back — not "provide a death benefit." Their language, not yours
  • Adjust mid-conversation: Clients aren't always purely one type. A Bull may become a Lamb when you bring up their kids. An Owl may relax into a Tiger once rapport is built. Stay flexible and keep reading the signals
  • Never lose yourself: Being a chameleon is about connection, not manipulation. Your core values, integrity, and professionalism stay constant. Only your style adapts

The golden rule

"People don't care how much you know until they know how much you care." The chameleon principle is ultimately about genuine empathy — meeting people where they are so they feel truly understood. That is what separates the top 1% from everyone else.

Writing Good Business & Avoiding Chargebacks
✅
Writing Good Business
Quality over Quantity
▾

What is "good business"?

Good business means policies that are accurately written, properly underwritten, and most importantly — that stay on the books. A placed policy that cancels within 9 months creates a chargeback. Your goal is not just to write policies, but to write policies that stick.

Accuracy in the application

  • Never guess on health information — ask every question on the application thoroughly
  • If a client has a condition, find the right carrier for that condition
  • Record the correct banking information. Verify routing and account number before submitting — one digit wrong causes NSF and chargeback
  • Confirm the draft date fits the client's pay schedule — Social Security clients always draft on the 5th or 6th
  • Ensure the client understands what they're buying, what they're paying, and when it drafts

Setting expectations

  • Tell the client what happens next — when they'll receive the policy, when the first draft happens, and what to expect on their bank statement
  • Clients who are surprised by their first draft are far more likely to cancel. Eliminate surprises
  • Explain the free-look period — knowing they have an out actually makes them less likely to use it

Book of Business discipline

  • Track every policy number, carrier, draft date, and premium in your Book of Business in this portal
  • Review your book monthly — flag policies approaching their 9-month mark that might be at risk
  • Reach out proactively to clients before renewals and at key life milestones
⚠️
Understanding & Avoiding Chargebacks
Protect Your Income
▾

What is a chargeback?

A chargeback occurs when a policy cancels within the advance period (usually 9–12 months). Since you were paid an advance commission upfront, the carrier claws back a prorated amount. A single chargeback can wipe out weeks of income.

Common causes

  • NSF / failed draft: Wrong banking information. The most common cause — always verify twice
  • Buyer's remorse: Client didn't fully understand what they bought
  • No follow-up: Agent disappears after the sale. Stay in touch
  • Wrong product: Client was sold something they didn't need or couldn't afford
  • Carrier rescission: Inaccurate health information on the application

How to avoid chargebacks

  • Verify banking info on every application — read it back to the client
  • Call within 48 hours to confirm welcome letter received
  • Call again when the first draft hits — proactively
  • If a draft fails, call the same day. Offer to update banking immediately
  • Build genuine relationships — clients who like you don't cancel on you
  • Never misrepresent a product, premium, or coverage terms

If a chargeback happens

Contact the carrier immediately. Some carriers allow policy replacement if the client re-enrolls. If it's a banking issue, resubmit with corrected info. Act fast — most chargebacks can be resolved if caught early.

Manager Training — Leadership & Team Development
👑
How to Become a True Leader
Manager Tier
▾

Leadership vs. Management

Management is a title. Leadership is earned. Your agents don't work for you — they work with you. Your job is to create an environment where they can succeed, and to show them daily why success is possible.

Lead by example

  • Your agents watch everything you do. If you're not writing business, neither will they
  • Be the first one on the phones and the last one to make excuses
  • Share your wins and losses with equal transparency — authenticity builds trust faster than success

Seek to understand first

  • When an agent is struggling, ask questions before offering solutions. "What's getting in your way right now?"
  • Every agent has a different motivation. Know what drives each person personally
  • Active listening is your most powerful leadership tool

Inspiration and incentives

  • Money motivates short-term. Purpose motivates long-term. Connect your team's work to the bigger mission
  • Celebrate publicly, coach privately
  • Create a culture of celebration — leaderboards, daily wins in team chat, shout-outs for first sales
  • Tie incentives to activity, not just results. Reward the behaviors that produce results

Catering your message to each agent

  • Apply the BOLT framework to your team — your Bull agents need direct feedback, your Lamb agents need encouragement
  • Have individual weekly check-ins — one-on-one time shows agents they matter individually
  • Adapt your coaching to where each agent is in their journey. A new agent needs step-by-step guidance; a 6-month agent needs challenge and accountability
Executive Training — Scaling & Operations
📊
Metric Performance & Business Forecasting
Executive Tier
▾

Key metrics every executive tracks

  • Issued AP: Total annualized premium of placed policies
  • Placement rate: Below 70% signals underwriting or application quality issues
  • Persistency rate: Policies still active after 13 months. 80%+ is industry standard
  • Agent activity ratio: How many agents wrote business this week vs total contracted. Healthy ratio is 60%+
  • Recruiting pipeline: New agents submitted, approved, contracted, and actively writing

Forecasting your business

  • Set monthly AP targets for each team and track weekly progress
  • Build a 90-day rolling forecast — know what's in the pipeline, what's placed, and what's at chargeback risk
  • Use persistency data to predict chargeback exposure and protect your commission income

Scaling to other states

  • Research non-resident license requirements for your target states — most require a non-resident application and fees
  • Identify high-opportunity markets: states with aging populations, low insurance penetration
  • Build virtual sales systems before expanding — if you can't manage agents remotely, scaling creates chaos
  • Contract agents who are already resident-licensed in target states

Recruiting agencies & downline executives

  • The most scalable move is recruiting agents who want to build teams, not just write business
  • Position LLFG as an ownership stake — they build their book, their team, and their legacy
  • Target insurance professionals frustrated with their current FMO's support, commission levels, or culture
  • Create a systematic onboarding process so every new agent gets the same high-quality experience from day one
